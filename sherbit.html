As people track health, financial and social lives on apps, Sherbit automatically aggregates users' personal data to answer more complex questions. Instead of "How many steps did I take today," Sherbit users can ask "How does music affect how many steps I take?"

While in alpha phase, Sherbit asked our team to create a clear value proposition, gain user insight, and create an onboarding process.

Project Scope

  • Understand main use cases
  • Present data to be more relevant
  • Streamline user flow
  • Lessen the learning curve

Deliverable

  • Presentation deck of our research and recommendations
  • Wireframes of our recommended designs

My Role

  • User Research
  • User Experience Design

Context

Sherbit’s founders are the Robin Hoods in our data mining world. In today's data mining world, Sherbit envisions users empowered with their own data and understanding their behaviorial patterns. How does location affect my spending? What type of weather encourages the most exercise? or the best sleep?

Screens of current app

However, even with powerful capabilities, Sherbit still needs to clarify how users will interact with the app and what data is most relevant. Were there enough userful takeaways? And, were the findings clear and actionable enough to use everyday?

Our team of three UX designers were excited to tackle this project.

Research

To help answer the Sherbit team's questions, we kicked off a big research effort which included literature reviews, competitive analyses, user interviews and usability tests.

Literature Reviews

Coming fresh into the world of personal data tracking, our team found many rich insights from academic journals, articles, blogs and the Quantified Self movement.

Emotions ultimately dictate how we make decisions, no matter how much we deliberate over the data. A combination of emotion and data solicits the strongest decisive responses.

Tracking data immediately heightens consciousness of goals and behavior. Once a user sees progress, they will try exponentially harder to achieve their objective.

As with the Quantified Self community, most people only track behaviors if they have a goal in mind.

Competitive Analyses

From our analysis, we realized that Sherbit is in the wild west of personal data tracking. No app is leading in this arena.

Along with a few apps we found, we compared a list of the top 50 most downloaded apps related to personal data tracking and saw:

The competitor with the most similar product was also in alpha development.

Competitors in the current market, like myFitness or Mint, track very siloed behaviors.

With only 9% of smartphone owners have wearables, apps are not very differentiated and have preliminary value propositions.

User Interviews

The user interviews were the juiciest part of our research process. For an app with such heavy personalization, gathering user insights was worth our due diligence.

Our team distributed an online survey to local universities and social platforms, like Reddit, Craigslist and Facebook. Gathering over 70 responses, we mapped out respondents according to how actively they engage in social apps, data tracking and goals setting.

Naturally, a few patterns emerged, and we were able to draw four provisional personas:

The Techie

Very engaged with social apps and tech. Uses data tracking out of interest in tech wearables.

The Achiever

Various engagment with social apps and tech. Very engaged with tracking personal data and setting goals.

The Aspirer

Moderately engaged with social apps, tech and data tracking. Indicated regularly setting goals.

The Socialite

Very engaged with social apps and tech. Indicated interest data tracking though only moderately engaged with it.

We targeted users from each persona group to test our hypotheses, to learn how each persona group tracked goals and to see how they interacted with Sherbit's current structure. Our interview broke down into three parts: behavioral questions, a card sort and a usability test. Over a week, we interviewed over a dozen people, gathering a lot of insight.

"I don't have a specific goal. I set a budget on 'fun stuff' so I can feel okay about treating myself when I stay under budget."
"I would love to track something like chronic pain and discover what's causing it. It could be so many things, and tracking could help me see through the noise."
"I already knew I wasn’t getting enough sleep, and now this confirms that and visualises it – but it doesn’t do anything to do to me. . .it should mean something."

Recording interesting observations onto sticky notes, we arranged behavioral insights into an affinity map and usabilty notes onto printouts of current screens.

(left) In the card sort, observing which datapoints people selected and how they decided, helped us imagine the way users would approach Sherbit. (middle) This affinity map helped us find themes in how users use apps, incorporate tech into their habits, set goals and track behavior. (right)Insights from the usability test helped us circumvent potential painpoints, which we annotated on print outs of Sherbit screens.

Overall themes were:

People use their phones to check for notifications and computers for longer errands. They have a high expectancy of quick, at-a-glance browsing.

Most people who track data, use systems that automatically input data, like Mint.com. A few manually input track their data. Otherwise, many say they mentally track and have a sense their behaviorial patterns.

Most goals were fueled by a sense of what they ought to be doing or what would reduce stress. These seemed tied to an emotive place where they can feel better about themselves.

The learning curve for this app is very high. Most people were reluctant to try and asked many questions. Once they tried, they could easily think of at least three more they'd like to create.

Most people ended up choosing one main datapoint, usually an emotive one like stress or mood, and correlated it other datapoints.

Across the board, people said that though the graphs were beautiful, they were ultimately difficult to explain.

There's a lack of interactivity. People kept tapping to find a data breakdown for the graph. They wanted to edit data for misinformation and to see data in different graphs.

A lack of actionable steps lost some people's attention. People are generally aware of their behaviors and would rather learn ways to change behavior.

Define

Defining personas and design principles grounded us as we brainstormed and developed solutions. We used them to gather inspiration and to make strategic tough calls during the design development phase.

Personas

We highlighted characteristics and stories from our interviews to craft four distinct personas.

The first two have more uses cases and motives to use Sherbit, and they are most likely to be first adopters. The latter two personas will use Sherbit out of curiosity and will use as Sherbit gains more traction.

Persona 1 is a techie with a strong itch to experiment with new products; Persona 2, a fast-paced professional looking for more control over their goals. Persona 3 has general aspirations for improvement, and Persona 4 uses mainstream tech.

User Journeys

We took common uses cases from our user interviews to create 12 user journeys. This was incredibly helpful in fleshing out many painpoints we hadn't considered.

We created three user journeys for ecah persona type.

Design Principles

Based on the needs we heard from users, we established three guiding principles to anchor our ideation process:

  • Be readable at a glance.
  • Be customizable for people's different methods of organization.
  • Be as actionable as a to do list.

Ideate

Now that we had gathered insights and defined our personas, we needed to distill the common and varient needs we were solving for. Referencing our journey maps and use cases, our team listed out each persona's needs, desire and painpoint.

Matrix of user needs

These notes were then condensed into nine common painpoints emerging across the persona groups. Now, our job was to sketch out ideas that help each persona address these painpoints.

Brainstorm

We sketched out ideas on our own before sharing them with the group. Our pitches described which painpoint and persona we were solving for. These, again, were organized into a chart according to which painpoint and persona the idea solved for.

What can I say? We're a chart-happy team.

We voted on the top three solutions to explore further. When selecting ideas, I wanted to avoid band-aid fixes, focus on innovation and address the pressing faults within the app.

Pressed for time, each team member spearheaded one recommendation to develop into a wireframe. We would present and reiterate on our ideas as a group. Also, we created a recommendations document with a handful of promising ideas and sketches that didn't make the cut.

Our team busily working on our ideas. Andy working on the user flow (left) and my paper prototype sketching out some ideas (right)

Design

In the end, these were our three recommendations developed into wireframe form. While still in low-fidelity, they addressed user painpoints and communicated solutions to our client's challenges.

#1: Onboarding Process

Our team decided that the onboarding was critical to lessen the learning curve. Users needed a basic overview and, most of all, a little hand holding. Our on-boarding process briefly describes the product benefits, walks users through making their first recipe and drops users off at the dashboard, the main screen of interaction.

User flow of the onboarding process

#2: Dashboard of Insights

Changing the dashboard was our biggest move. We needed a way to help users get the insights quickly as they check the app throughout the day or week.

Now, the dashboard is insight-driven. Users can design their dashboard with relevant, insight-focused data. If they are curious for more information, they can swipe further for detailed graphs and then again click into graphs for more information.

The dashboard slowly reveals more information as users swipe into the insights.

Storyboard of a potential use case

#3 Customizable Data

An early prototype of our dashboard, showing the potential user flow


We also added more features to give users more control over their dashboard. They can delete, add and rearrange insights and graphs.

Also, we designed the dashboard to easily add pre-existing recipes relevant to their insight. This helps improve the reliability of the insight and educates users of different possible ways to manipulate their data.

Testing

We were tested our prototype with dozens of friends and colleagues to iterate and validate our user flow and microinteractions. Had we more time, I'd love to design out a high-fidelity prototype to test with potential users. This is a critical step that Sherbit's team will tackle.

Conclusion

Our clients loved it. They even called us three times after our presentation with kind words for our final deliverable.

Working on Sherbit has been an incredibly gratifying project. It was an honor to work with such a talented and ambitious client. Moreover, a researcher at heart, I enjoyed unearthing insights from talking and observing users.Within a short three week timeframe, we were able to give recommendations that address honest user needs and offer delightful experiences.

Sherbit was a big growing experience for me. If our team was to continue working on Sherbit, I would like to explore more of the ideas we brainstormed, take our product to a higher fidelity and perform much more user testing. I'd like to bring our prototype to each persona group to test our prototype.

All in all, I appreciated our client's investment in our research and our team's creativity and tenacity to deliver user centered recommendations.
